Classical Rutherford scattering and quantum Coulomb wave picture

Physical model

This page visualizes alpha particles approaching a heavy Coulomb target. The large panel shows the classical beam and scattered particles. The two right panels compare the classical Rutherford and quantum Coulomb differential cross sections, and show the real part of an incident plane wave plus an outgoing Coulomb scattered wave.

$$\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}=\left(\frac{k_e Z_1 Z_2}{4E}\right)^2\frac{1}{\sin^4(\theta/2)},\qquad |f(\theta)|^2=\frac{\eta^2}{4k^2\sin^4(\theta/2)}$$

Settings

Default settings use an alpha particle, $Z_1=2$, gold target, $Z_2=79$, and kinetic energy $E=5\,\mathrm{MeV}$. Change the controls below and press simulate again. Pure Coulomb scattering gives the same angular dependence in the classical and quantum differential cross sections.

Parameter–Scattering Summary

The table below summarizes how the controls change the visible particle scattering, cross section, and wave picture.

Parameter Main physical role Classical particle picture Quantum / cross-section picture
Projectile charge Z₁ Sets the Coulomb repulsion strength together with Z₂. Larger Z₁ bends particles more strongly, especially for small impact parameter. The differential cross section scales as (Z₁Z₂)²; the Sommerfeld parameter η also increases.
Target charge Z₂ Represents the nuclear charge of the scattering center. A heavier charge such as gold produces visibly larger deflection angles. Both classical Rutherford and quantum Coulomb curves rise with Z₂².
Energy E Controls how easily the projectile is deflected by the Coulomb field. Higher energy makes trajectories straighter; lower energy gives wider scattering. The cross section scales as 1/E². The wave number k increases with √E.
Impact parameter b Distance between incoming trajectory and target center. Small b gives large scattering angle; large b gives near-forward scattering. The angular distribution is sharply forward-peaked because of the sin⁻⁴(θ/2) factor.
Particle count Controls the visual sampling of many incoming impact parameters. More particles make the scattering cone and forward peak easier to see. Does not change the formula; it only changes the animation density.

Rule of thumb: charge product Z₁Z₂ strengthens scattering, energy suppresses scattering, and small impact parameter produces the largest deflection. In the quantum panel, θ is measured from the incident beam direction and is the same angle appearing in f(θ) and dσ/dΩ = |f(θ)|².
