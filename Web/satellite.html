Physical model

This interactive page visualizes an inclined geosynchronous satellite. The left panel shows Earth, the compressed satellite orbit, the sub-satellite point, and the observer line of sight. The two right panels show the zoomed ground track and the sky path seen by the observer.

Settings

Default settings: observer at Taipei-like coordinates, nominal GEO longitude $121.565^\circ$, inclination $15^\circ$, eccentricity $0$, and argument of perigee $270^\circ$. Change the controls below and press simulate again.

Parameter–Trajectory Shape Summary

The table below summarizes how each control parameter changes the ground-track analemma and the sky path seen by the observer. It is meant as a quick guide for interpreting the simulation results.

Parameter Main effect when changed Ground-track shape Observer sky path
Inclination i Sets the satellite declination range, which controls the north–south motion. For i = 0°, the sub-satellite point is nearly fixed. As i increases, the upper and lower lobes of the figure-eight become taller, with latitude range approximately ±i. The sky path stretches more strongly north–south. High-latitude observers may see a larger elevation variation.
Eccentricity e Makes the orbital angular speed non-uniform, producing east–west longitude oscillation. For e = 0, the analemma is mostly symmetric. Larger e increases the left–right width and can make the two lobes asymmetric or tilted. The sky path widens east–west. The satellite appears to linger near some directions longer than others.
Argument of perigee ω Controls the phase of the eccentricity-driven east–west motion relative to the north–south motion. Changes the twist direction and the location of left–right asymmetry in the figure-eight. A common setting, ω = 270°, gives a typical analemma orientation. Changes the tilt of the sky path. The timing of the highest point and the easternmost and westernmost points shifts.
Nominal satellite longitude λₛ Shifts the center longitude of the entire ground track. The figure-eight shape is mostly unchanged, but the whole track moves east or west, centered near λₛ. Relative to the observer, the path shifts toward the eastern or western sky. The elevation range may also increase or decrease.
Observer latitude φₒ Changes the observer’s geometric position relative to the equator and the satellite track. Does not change the satellite ground track itself, but moves the observer-latitude line shown on the plot. At higher latitudes, a GEO satellite usually appears lower in the southern or northern sky. The elevation range decreases, and part of the path may fall below the horizon.
Observer longitude λₒ Changes the longitude difference between the observer and the satellite’s central longitude. Does not change the ground-track figure-eight itself; it only changes the observer’s position relative to the track. A larger longitude difference places the satellite farther east or west in the sky and usually lowers its elevation. If the difference is too large, the satellite may not be visible.

Rule of thumb: inclination mainly controls the vertical height, eccentricity mainly controls the horizontal width, and argument of perigee controls the phase relation between the two.
